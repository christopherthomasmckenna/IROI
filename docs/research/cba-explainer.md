## What this calculator does, in one paragraph

When a criminal case is diverted from the traditional Criminal Justice System
(CJS) into Restorative Justice Conferencing (RJC), two things happen: the
government spends money differently (an RJC program instead of prosecution,
courtrooms, and incarceration), and people's lives turn out differently (more
restitution paid, less time lost, better earnings prospects, less repeat
offending). **Cost-benefit analysis (CBA) puts both of those in the same
currency — dollars — so they can be compared directly.** This calculator does
that per case: it totals what a case costs down each path, adds the measurable
dollar benefits to harmed parties, the responsible party, and the community,
and expresses the result as a ratio — the Impact ROI.

CBA is a standard, widely used tool in justice policy. The Vera Institute
defines it as "an economic tool that compares the costs of programs or policies
with the benefits they deliver," whose hallmark is that "costs and benefits are
expressed in monetary terms so that they can be directly compared"
([Vera, *Cost-Benefit Analysis and Justice Policy Toolkit*, 2014](https://vera-institute.files.svdcdn.com/production/downloads/publications/cba-justice-policy-toolkit.pdf)).

## How to read a number like "11×"

An Impact ROI of 11× means: **for every $1 invested in the RJC program, about
$11 comes back in combined savings and social benefit.** This is the same way
public-policy ROI results are normally phrased — for example, Pew's Results
First model describes a Colorado program as returning "$13.20 in benefits for
every $1 spent"
([Pew Results First](https://www.pew.org/en/research-and-analysis/fact-sheets/2019/05/results-first-cost-benefit-model-aids-policymakers-in-funding-decisions)).

The calculator builds the ratio in four steps, visible on every summary page:

1. **Program savings** — what the CJS path would have cost, minus what the RJC
   path costs.
2. **Total benefits** — the dollar value of outcomes for harmed parties
   (restitution, avoided expenses), the responsible party (earnings from
   avoided incarceration, GED, expungement), and the community (community
   service, recovered taxes, reduced recidivism).
3. **Overall benefit** — savings plus benefits.
4. **Impact ROI** — overall benefit divided by the RJC cost per case.

**An analogy:** spending $1,000 insulating your attic doesn't hand you cash —
but it quietly returns several times that in heating bills you never pay. An
11× ratio means each program dollar works like that: it prevents future costs —
court dates, jail days, victim losses — worth about eleven dollars.

## Where the dollars come from — and an honest distinction

Not all "benefit dollars" are the same, and trustworthy CBAs say so plainly.

- **Budgetary savings** are dollars a government could actually stop spending —
  prosecution time, courtroom operations, jail days.
- **Monetized social benefits** are dollar values assigned to real outcomes
  that don't arrive as checks — a victim's avoided lost income, a young
  person's improved earnings, harm from crimes that never happen.

Both are real value. But only the first kind can ever show up in a county
budget — and even it comes with a subtlety: diverting *one* case doesn't
immediately shrink a courthouse's spending. The U.S. National Institute of
Justice notes that avoided individual arrests and hearings "do not yield
direct benefits to the criminal justice system" until caseloads change enough
to affect staffing
([NIJ](https://nij.ojp.gov/topics/articles/cost-benefit-analysis-criminal-justice-reforms)).

**An analogy:** if a smoke detector prevents a house fire, the avoided damage
is completely real — but no one mails you a check for it. Most of the dollars
in an ROI figure are like that: real value, measured in avoided harm.

**Another:** if one passenger skips a flight, the airline doesn't save the
average ticket's worth of fuel and crew — the plane flies anyway. Diverting
one case works the same way: savings at the "average cost per case" level
become real budget relief only when enough cases are diverted to change
dockets, staffing, or beds. (This model uses average per-case costs — the
honest, obtainable numbers — so its savings should be read as value created,
not as next year's budget line. Research finds the *marginal* cost of one
case is often less than half the average
([Wilson & Lemoine 2022](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/08874034211060336)).)

## Why three numbers instead of one

Every result in this calculator appears as **Low / Medium / High**. That's not
indecision — it's the field's standard honesty practice, called sensitivity
analysis. CJS costs vary enormously with jurisdiction and case path (how much
court time? any incarceration?), so a serious model shows the range those
assumptions produce. Vera's guidance is blunt: a CBA "will be misleading if
its results are calculated using only point estimates," and one that shows a
range of scenarios "will not only be more credible... but more informative"
([Vera toolkit, p. 27](https://vera-institute.files.svdcdn.com/production/downloads/publications/cba-justice-policy-toolkit.pdf)).
The gold-standard state model (WSIPP, Washington State) goes further, running
thousands of simulations and reporting the "chance benefits exceed costs"
([WSIPP](https://www.wsipp.wa.gov/BenefitCost/TechnicalDocumentation)).

**An analogy:** a forecaster who says "70% chance of rain" is being more
honest, not less confident, than one who promises sunshine. The Low/Medium/High
columns are that kind of honesty. If even the Low number clears 1×, the
conclusion is robust.

## Who pays, and who benefits

A distinctive strength of CBA — and a fair question for any reader — is
*perspective*. The program's costs are paid now, from one budget. The returns
arrive over time, spread across others: some to courts and corrections, much
of it to harmed parties, responsible parties, and the community at large.
Standard practice (Vera, WSIPP) is to disaggregate results by taxpayer,
victim/harmed party, and participant perspectives — which is exactly why this
calculator itemizes HP, RP, and Community benefits as separate, inspectable
sections rather than one merged number
([Vera toolkit, pp. 7, 29](https://vera-institute.files.svdcdn.com/production/downloads/publications/cba-justice-policy-toolkit.pdf)).

## Is the underlying idea sound? The evidence base

The economic case for RJC does not rest on this calculator alone:

- The UK Ministry of Justice's seven-year evaluation (Shapland et al.) found
  RJ reduced reoffending frequency by 14%, with 85% victim satisfaction, and
  reported roughly £9 in savings per £1 spent
  ([Restorative Justice Council summary](https://restorativejustice.org.uk/resources/evidence-supporting-use-restorative-justice)).
- The Campbell Collaboration systematic review of ten randomized trials found
  RJ conferencing produces "a modest but highly cost-effective reduction in
  the frequency of repeat offending," with benefit-to-cost ratios of 3.7–8.1×
  in the UK experiments
  ([Strang, Sherman et al. 2013](https://restorativejustice.org.uk/sites/default/files/resources/files/Campbell%20RJ%20review.pdf)).
- The strongest recent US evidence: San Francisco's *Make it Right* randomized
  trial of pre-charge RJC diversion for youth, published in *Econometrica*
  (2024), found large, persistent reductions in rearrest
  ([NBER w29150](https://www.nber.org/papers/w29150)).

Honest footnote: the UK Parliament's Justice Committee cautioned against
"undue reliance" on the headline £-per-£ statistic, because such ratios come
from specific programs and offense mixes
([Justice Committee 2016](https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/cm201617/cmselect/cmjust/164/16405.htm)).
That caution is precisely why this calculator exists: rather than importing
someone else's ratio, it lets each jurisdiction compute its own from local
costs — with every deviation from the Philadelphia defaults documented.

## What this model is careful about (and what it isn't)

Things a fair reader should know:

- **The ratio isn't the whole story.** Experts consider net benefit (dollars
  in minus dollars out) the sturdiest single statistic; ratios can shift with
  bookkeeping choices ([Vera toolkit, p. 28](https://vera-institute.files.svdcdn.com/production/downloads/publications/cba-justice-policy-toolkit.pdf)).
  The calculator shows the full derivation so both are visible.
- **A high ratio doesn't mean the program is free.** Program costs are real
  appropriated dollars now; much of the return accrues later and elsewhere.
- **Each dollar is counted once.** The model keeps cost avoidance and benefit
  categories distinct to avoid double counting, a classic CBA pitfall
  ([IDB, 10 Pitfalls in CBA](https://blogs.iadb.org/efectividad-desarrollo/en/10-pitfalls-in-cost-benefit-analysis/)).
- **It's a per-case, single-period model.** It does not discount multi-year
  benefit streams to present value (standard in long-horizon CBAs; effects are
  small at short horizons — [Vera toolkit, p. 24](https://vera-institute.files.svdcdn.com/production/downloads/publications/cba-justice-policy-toolkit.pdf)).
- **Some benefits aren't counted at all.** Victim healing, community trust,
  and procedural justice resist monetization; leaving them out likely biases
  the ratio *downward* — an omission that favors caution, not the program
  ([Vera toolkit, pp. 11, 20](https://vera-institute.files.svdcdn.com/production/downloads/publications/cba-justice-policy-toolkit.pdf)).
- **Results are local.** Importing another jurisdiction's result is exactly
  what Vera warns about; localize the inputs (the calculator's whole purpose)
  rather than borrowing the output.

1. [Vera Institute — Cost-Benefit Analysis and Justice Policy Toolkit (2014)](https://vera-institute.files.svdcdn.com/production/downloads/publications/cba-justice-policy-toolkit.pdf) — the best single plain-language, justice-specific walkthrough of CBA.

4. [Pew — Results First Cost-Benefit Model overview](https://www.pew.org/en/research-and-analysis/fact-sheets/2019/05/results-first-cost-benefit-model-aids-policymakers-in-funding-decisions) — two-page plain-English description of state ROI modeling.
5. [Strang, Sherman et al. — Campbell Systematic Review of RJ Conferencing (2013)](https://restorativejustice.org.uk/sites/default/files/resources/files/Campbell%20RJ%20review.pdf) — the definitive RCT evidence base for RJC.
